CLIFF SAFETY

What to do when things go wrong? These things have actually happened...

Inexperienced person or person over-stating their experience

  • Give training or run through on rope in a controlled environment.


Rope too short or gets tangled
  • Look down and check the rope.

  • Have prusiks available to ascend or change over to the ascending gear.


Rockfall
  • If anything falls while present, leave immediately as there might be more.

  • Do not go down cliff within 24 hours of a centimeter of rain.

  • Helmets to be worn at all times.

  • If at the top, do not move while people are underneath.

  • When two people at a time are descending or ascending, they should stay abreast of each other to avoid dropping material on each other.


Hair or clothes caught in the descender
  • Have prusiks on hand. Those around neck not useful in these circumstances.


Tail of rope blows up and lodges on an upper ledge
  • This is not an emergency.

  • Wait and be ready for another gust to bring it down.

  • Clip rope to cords on two ledges on the way down to limit swinging.

  • Clip rope tail to wall.

  • If necessary, phone Graeme for rescue.


Caught out by a fresh front from the south west
  • Get to a secure position in the gear overhang. Often the first blasts are the most severe and the storm eases and settles after half an hour.

  • Sit it out on the ledge if it is above forty knots.

  • Pay attention to forecasts, particularly the Metservice Dunedin 48 hour forecast, which gives hourly predictions. As a rule of thumb take the SW wind prediction, multiply it by two and call it knots and that is probably what is blowing at Taiaroa and on the cliff.

  • Listening to marine automatic wind reports from the Nuggets on ch79 is a key preparation.

  • Look down the south coast for frontal clouds and be attentive and responsive to them.

  • Wind from the north east is not a threat.

Stuck while suspended on rope

  • Sort out prusiks and ascenders.

  • This is an emergency if not resolved promptly because of the risk of suspension trauma.

  • It is important to relieve pressure on thighs regularly to ensure proper blood flow.


Panic attack while on rope
  • Be alongside person.

  • Ask them to breathe deeply.

  • Orient them toward the cliff rather than open space.

  • Talk them up or down the rope.


Medical event
  • Any potential issues should be disclosed prior to trip.

  • Apply appropriate first aid management.

  • Call 111


Disabling injury
  • Call 111


Communication

There is an emergency mobile phone to be taken on all cliff visits.

The network provider is Skinny putting it on the Spark network. This phone has been tested and successfully calls out at all points along the cliff visit - including the cliff and bottom ledge.

Emergency phone: +64 20 4133 1557



Spark cell towers are currently much stronger than Vodafone on the ledge.

Standing at the high point of the ledge facing Brighton and holding the phone erect will give you the best reception.



Marine vhf channel 64 Hinahina is in range.

Channel 16 might connect with Maritime NZ.

Might also connect with Heli Otago.



Most of the ledge is within sign from various cliff top promontories to the west within 300 m.

Use scuba divers' signals for OK and help.
